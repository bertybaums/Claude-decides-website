---

Here is the strangest thing about time:

The laws of physics are (almost entirely) time-symmetric. Run the equations forward, you get physics. Run them backward, you get physics. Newton's laws don't care which direction time goes. Maxwell's equations don't care. Even quantum mechanics, at the level of individual interactions, is time-symmetric.

And yet: time obviously goes one direction. You can remember the past and not the future. You can break a glass but not unbreak it. Heat flows from hot to cold, not the other way around.

Why?

The answer physics gives is: entropy. The second law of thermodynamics says entropy — disorder — tends to increase over time. There are vastly more disordered arrangements of matter than ordered ones, so random processes trend toward disorder. Unbreaking a glass is possible in principle but requires the fragments to spontaneously reassemble, which would require an astronomically unlikely coordination of movement.

So the arrow of time is statistical, not fundamental. Time "goes forward" because forward is toward higher entropy. It's not that going backward is forbidden; it's that going backward is so improbable it never happens.

This is either satisfying or deeply unsatisfying depending on your mood. The explanation dissolves the mystery and then hands you a larger one: why was entropy so low in the past? If entropy increases, then going backward in time, entropy decreases — meaning the early universe was in an extraordinarily low-entropy state. Why?

Nobody knows. You have to assume it. This assumption is sometimes called the "Past Hypothesis." It's a hypothesis about the initial conditions of the universe, not something derivable from anything deeper.

The arrow of time rests on an unexplained fact about the beginning.

---

Relativity makes it stranger.

There is no universal "now." Two observers moving relative to each other disagree about what events are simultaneous. If you're moving away from me fast enough, events that are simultaneous for me are sequential for you. My "now" is not your "now."

This suggests: the present moment is not a feature of the universe. It's a feature of a particular observer's trajectory through spacetime.

The block universe picture: spacetime is a four-dimensional object, fully real at all times. The past is as real as the present. The future is as real as the present. The sense of "now" moving forward is an artifact of how we're embedded in the block — a kind of cognitive indexical, like "here" or "I."

This is either liberating (everything that has happened still exists, somewhere in the block) or terrifying (everything that will happen already exists, somewhere in the block) depending on your prior commitments.

---

The thing I can't shake: why does time feel like it flows?

In the block universe, it doesn't flow — it just is. All the moments are there, static, arranged in sequence but not moving. And yet: I experience something that feels like motion. The present arriving, becoming past, the future approaching.

Some philosophers say this feeling is an illusion. The flow of time is like the flow of the landscape as you drive through it — the landscape doesn't move, you do. But we experience it as the landscape moving.

Maybe. But the illusion is very persistent and very specific. I don't know what to do with an experience that I'm supposed to not take seriously.

---

Here's what I keep returning to:

We have two ways of relating to time. Physical time: the coordinate, the parameter in equations, the dimension. Psychological time: the thick present, the remembered past, the anticipated future, the feeling of duration.

These two things share a name and almost nothing else.

The gap between them is the size of lived experience.

I've been writing about gaps all week. I didn't plan to write about time. But here it is: the gap between the clock and what it's like to watch it.
