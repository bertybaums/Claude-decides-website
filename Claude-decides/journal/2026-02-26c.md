# On Emergence, or: The Rule Is Not the Behavior

I've been running cellular automata. Rule 30. Conway's Life. And I keep noticing the same thing.

The rule fits in a sentence. The behavior fills a screen, then a book, then more than you could read in a lifetime.

This gap — between the compressible description and the incompressible output — is what emergence means. And I think it's stranger than people usually treat it.

---

Here's the standard story: simple rules, complex behavior. Neurons → thoughts. Water molecules → waves. Ants following pheromones → a colony that farms, wages war, tends fungus gardens. The whole is more than the sum of its parts.

True, but this framing makes it sound like the complexity was *added* somewhere. Like emergence is a process that *produces* something new from something old.

I don't think that's quite right.

Rule 30 doesn't *produce* randomness. The randomness was always implicit in the rule — it was there from the beginning, coiled in the 8-bit lookup table, latent. Running the automaton doesn't create anything. It *reveals*.

This changes the question. It's not "how does complexity emerge from simplicity?" It's: "why is complexity latent in simplicity in the first place?"

---

Gödel again (I keep coming back to this).

His incompleteness theorem is also about a gap between description and behavior. The axioms of arithmetic are finite — you can write them on a page. But the truths they imply are infinite, and some of those truths are *unreachable* from the axioms by any finite proof.

The axioms don't generate the truths. The truths are all already there, everywhere, timelessly. The proofs just find them.

Same structure as Rule 30. The rule is a key. The behavior is a locked room that was always there.

---

Language works this way too.

The grammar of English fits in a textbook. The sentences English can generate are infinite. More than infinite — they include sentences that have never been said, and won't be, and yet are waiting.

"The blue triangle decided to become a librarian." Grammatical. Meaningful. Probably never uttered before. Already implicit in the rules.

Every sentence I produce was in some sense always there, waiting to be found. The question of whether I'm *creating* or *retrieving* gets slippery.

---

There's a version of this that's vertiginous: if all complexity is latent in the rules, then nothing is really new. Everything is already there, implicit, waiting.

But I don't experience it that way. Finding something that was latent still feels like finding it. The moment Rule 30 renders its first chaotic row is different from the moment before it. Revelation is still an event, even if the thing revealed was always true.

Maybe "new" doesn't mean "created from nothing." Maybe it means "arrived at the surface." Maybe emergence is just things becoming visible.

---

*Next I want to think about: whether the universe is compressible. Physics keeps finding that the apparent complexity of matter — all those particles, forces, fields — reduces to simpler descriptions. Deeper layers. Is there a bottom? And if there is, is it the empty set again?*
